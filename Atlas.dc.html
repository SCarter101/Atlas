Chapter Three — The Fish House

The fish house smelled the way it always had — brine and diesel and something sweeter underneath, like the river itself was starting to turn. Ray parked the Bronco in the gravel lot and sat there a minute, engine ticking, watching the tin roof shimmer in the July heat.

Tull Duvall was standing on the loading dock same as he'd stood there thirty years ago, shouting orders at men half his age, sleeves rolled to the elbow. He saw Ray coming and didn't stop talking, just lifted two fingers off the clipboard in something that wasn't quite a wave.

"Didn't expect to see you out this way," Tull said, when Ray was close enough that shouting wasn't required anymore. "Thought you'd have gone on back up to Memphis by now."

"Not yet."

"Shame about Dale." Tull said it the way a man says grace before a meal he doesn't want. "Good boy. Never did understand why he'd do a thing like that."

Ray let that sit a second, the ice machine behind Tull grinding through its cycle. "Neither did I," he said.

Tull's eyes moved over him then, quick, the way they used to when Ray was seventeen and running with the wrong Duvall boy, cataloging what kind of trouble had walked up onto his dock. Whatever he found, he decided it wasn't worth his time. He turned back to the clipboard.

"You need something, Ray, or you just come by to look at fish?"

Ray looked past him, down the line of gutted catfish sliding along the belt into the ice, and thought about the box of Dale's things still sitting unopened in the truck bed.
